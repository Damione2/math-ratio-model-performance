\begin{abstract}
Large language models (LLMs) are sensitive to the structure and composition of their training data. While prior work has examined inference nondeterminism and data quality, the effect of domain‑specific data ratios on hallucination behavior remains underexplored. This paper studies how varying the proportion of mathematical examples in synthetic training datasets influences model accuracy, stability across random seeds, and hallucination patterns. Using a reproducible pipeline, we train models on datasets with controlled math‑to‑non‑math ratios and evaluate them with multi‑seed inference, bootstrap resampling, and a CfC‑based hallucination detector. Three figures summarize the core empirical evidence: the raw relationship with confidence bands, the bootstrap distribution of the slope estimate, and residual diagnostics for the mixed‑effects model.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Large language models have achieved strong performance across many tasks, yet their behavior depends critically on the distribution and composition of training data. Mathematics is a particularly informative domain for studying model behavior: it is symbolic, structured, and sensitive to small representational changes. This paper investigates how the \emph{ratio} of mathematical examples in training data affects downstream performance, stability, and hallucination tendencies.

We focus on five outcomes: (1) accuracy on math tasks, (2) general‑domain accuracy, (3) hallucination frequency, (4) stability across random seeds, and (5) uncertainty estimated via bootstrap resampling. The experiments and analysis are implemented in the accompanying GitHub repository (2026), which contains dataset generation scripts, training pipelines, evaluation code, and the CfC hallucination detector.

\section{Related work}
\subsection{Inference nondeterminism}
Engineering analyses have shown that LLM inference can be nondeterministic even at deterministic sampling settings due to backend kernel behavior and batch‑size dependencies. He et al. (Thinking Machines, 2025) document kernel‑level sources of nondeterminism and propose engineering fixes to reduce variability in inference outputs. While inference determinism is important for reproducibility, it does not by itself resolve hallucinations that arise from dataset composition or domain imbalance.

\subsection{Hallucination detection}
Detecting hallucinations remains an open problem. We adopt a CfC (Chain‑of‑Thought consistency) inspired detector that flags outputs inconsistent with domain constraints or unsupported by model internal evidence. This detector is effective at identifying domain‑inconsistent outputs and reasoning failures, and it is particularly useful for contrasting math and non‑math hallucination patterns.

\subsection{Dataset composition and domain balance}
Prior work on data quality and scaling laws emphasizes the role of diverse, high‑quality data. Less explored is the effect of \emph{domain ratios}—the relative proportion of examples from a specific domain—on model specialization, generalization, and hallucination. This study provides empirical evidence that domain ratios materially influence these behaviors.

\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Dataset construction}
We synthesize datasets with controlled math ratios \(r\in\{0,0.10,0.25,0.50,0.75,1.0\}\). Each dataset contains \(N\) total examples (constant across ratios) so that only composition varies. Math examples include symbolic problems, proof sketches, and structured reasoning prompts; non‑math examples include general knowledge, conversational, and commonsense reasoning prompts.

\subsection{Model training}
All models share the same architecture and hyperparameters to isolate the effect of dataset composition:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item identical model architecture and parameter count,
  \item same optimizer and learning rate schedule,
  \item same batch size and number of training steps,
  \item identical data augmentation and preprocessing.
\end{itemize}
Random seeds differ only for initialization and data shuffling when evaluating seed sensitivity.

\subsection{Evaluation protocol}
\paragraph{Multi‑seed inference}
For each trained model we run inference across \(S\) random seeds to measure variability in outputs, accuracy, and hallucination counts.

\paragraph{Bootstrap resampling}
We apply bootstrap resampling to compute confidence intervals for accuracy and hallucination metrics. For each model and metric we draw \(B\) bootstrap samples and report the empirical 95\% intervals.

\paragraph{Hallucination detection}
A CfC‑based detector flags outputs that violate domain constraints or contain unsupported claims. We compute hallucination rate as the fraction of flagged outputs per evaluation set.

\subsection{Statistical modeling}
We fit a linear mixed‑effects model to quantify the effect of math ratio \(r\) on outcomes while accounting for seed and dataset noise. For outcome \(y_{ij}\) (e.g., accuracy or hallucination rate) for seed \(i\) and dataset \(j\):


\[
y_{ij} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 r_j + u_i + \epsilon_{ij},
\]


where \(u_i\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_u^2)\) captures seed‑level random effects and \(\epsilon_{ij}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)\) is residual noise. Significance of \(\beta_1\) indicates a systematic effect of math ratio.

\section{Results}
\subsection{Descriptive summary}
Models trained with higher math ratios show improved performance on math benchmarks and reduced math‑task hallucinations, while general‑domain performance tends to decline. Mixed datasets (e.g., \(r=0.25\)–\(0.50\)) typically yield the most stable behavior across seeds.

\subsection{Interpretation}
Figure~\ref{fig:scatter} shows a negative relationship between math ratio and best F1: as the fraction of math examples increases, best F1 on the evaluated tasks tends to decline. Figure~\ref{fig:bootstrap} quantifies uncertainty in the slope estimate; the 95\% bootstrap interval excludes zero, indicating a robust negative effect under the sampling scheme used. Figure~\ref{fig:mixedlm_resid} shows residuals from the mixed‑effects model; the lack of strong structure supports the model choice and suggests that seed‑level random effects capture important variance.

\section{Discussion}
The experiments demonstrate that dataset composition—specifically the math example ratio—substantially shapes model behavior. Mathematical examples provide structure that improves domain performance but can induce brittle behavior outside that domain. This specialization manifests as increased hallucination in general‑domain contexts and greater sensitivity to seed and sampling noise when ratios are extreme.

The Thinking Machines analysis of inference nondeterminism (He, 2025) underscores the importance of deterministic inference for reproducibility. Our results complement that perspective: even with deterministic inference, dataset imbalance can produce systematic hallucination patterns. Effective mitigation therefore requires a two‑pronged approach: (1) engineering fixes to reduce inference nondeterminism, and (2) dataset‑level strategies to maintain domain balance and prevent harmful specialization.

\section{Conclusion}
Varying the proportion of mathematical examples in training data has clear, measurable effects on LLM accuracy, stability, and hallucination behavior. Balanced datasets (moderate math ratios) produce the most stable models, while extreme ratios lead to specialization and increased hallucination outside the target domain. Future work should explore adaptive sampling, curriculum learning, and domain‑aware hallucination detectors to jointly address inference determinism and dataset composition.
